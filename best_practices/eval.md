#### Define What Good Look Likes Along The Way:
Most LLM evaluation tools ask you to define what "good" looks like upfront. But here's the problem: when you're just starting to evaluate AI outputs, you don't always know what patterns matter yet. You just know something feels off. You start by reviewing AI outputs and documenting what went wrong. No predefined categories. No forcing failures into boxes that don't fit. Just honest observations about what broke. Then you group those observations into error categories and see which problems occur most often. Suddenly you have data on what to fix first. Not what you thought would be important. What actually is important. Once you understand your error patterns, you turn that analysis into a deployable evaluation. The intuition you built while reviewing outputs becomes the automated check on your AI product that runs at scale.

Evaluation: The Indispensable Moat for LLM Applications
Building successful applications powered by LLMs requires more than just clever prompting or chaining models together. The "moat," lies in rigorous, systematic evaluation. While traditional software testing relies on deterministic checks for predictable logic, evaluating LLM systems is fundamentally different. the environment is variable, the system is non-deterministic, and "good" performance is nuanced. Simple latency or pass/fail checks are insufficient. We need thoughtful, intentional evaluations to understand if our AI product truly works and to guide its improvement. The goal isn't just to launch, but to iterate effectively, prevent regressions, assess capabilities, ensure alignment with desired behavior, and make informed comparisons between different approaches.

### “Perfect Eval” is Flawed: It's all about data distribution and regression
In AI, a perfect score is a red flag. This happens when a model has inadvertently been trained on eval-data or prompts that are too similar to tests. If you are sure your data is clean but you’re still getting perfect accuracy, chances are your test is too weak (not diverse and real) or not measuring what matters. Tests that always pass don’t help you improve. When your models have perfect scores, you lose the ability to differentiate between them. The goal of evaluations isn’t to pat yourself on the back for a perfect score. It’s to uncover areas for improvement and ensure your AI is truly solving the problems it’s meant to address. Evals aren't metrics they are development methodology.

### Evaluation In Application Patterns
The specific evaluation techniques you prioritize will depend on your application:
- Simple Task Apps (Classification, Extraction): Evaluation often focuses on traditional metrics like accuracy, precision, and recall against a labeled dataset using code-based checks.
- Free-Text Generation (Summarization, Chatbots): These heavily rely on LLM-as-judge and human evaluation to assess relevance, coherence, factuality, safety, and tone, as simple code checks are often insufficient.
- Multi-Turn Chat: Evaluation must consider the entire conversation. Key aspects include consistency across turns, accurate context recall, and successful completion of tasks initiated earlier. A practical approach is often to evaluate the entire interaction with a binary pass/fail (did it achieve the goal without major errors?), supplemented by error analysis to pinpoint failures in specific turns or capabilities (like context handling) which can then be targeted with more specific evals.
- AI Agents: Agents introduce significant complexity, requiring multi-level evaluation. You need component-level evals: Did it choose the right tool? Was the API call structured correctly? Was the retrieved information relevant? Was the final response appropriately synthesized from the context? Was the plan logical? Alongside these, end-to-end task success evaluation is critical: Did the agent ultimately accomplish the user's goal? Analyzing the agent's trajectory (the sequence of steps taken) compared to an optimal path can also provide insights into efficiency and reasoning quality. 

### Practical Evals for Long Running Agents: Eval Driven Development
The capabilities that make AI agents useful—autonomy, intelligence, and flexibility—also make them harder to evaluate. Single-turn evaluations are straightforward: a prompt, a response, and grading logic. Agent evaluations are even more complex. Agents use tools across many turns, modifying state in the environment and adapting as they go—which means mistakes can propagate and compound.

#### Consistency Matters:
Sometimes, what we want to measure is how often (what proportion of the trials) an agent succeeds for a task.
pass@k measures the likelihood that an agent gets at least one correct solution in k attempts. As k increases, pass@k score rises: more “shots on goal” means higher odds of at least 1 success.
pass^k measures the probability that all k trials succeed. As k increases, pass^k falls since demanding consistency across more trials is a harder bar to clear. This metric especially matters for customer-facing agents where users expect reliable behavior every time.
#### A roadmap to great evals for agents
Eval-driven agent development: define success early, measure it clearly, and iterate continuously. 
Start early: We see teams delay building evals because they think they need hundreds of tasks. In reality, 20-50 simple tasks drawn from real failures is a great start. In early agent development, each change to the system often has a clear, noticeable impact, and this large effect size means small sample sizes suffice. It’s best to take the 80/20 approach in the beginning.
Start with what you already test manually: If you're already in production, look at your bug tracker and support queue. Converting user-reported failures into test cases ensures your suite reflects actual usage; With traces that you read and know the answer.
Write tasks with clear reference solutions: A good task is one where two domain experts would independently reach the same pass/fail verdict. Could they pass the task themselves? If not, the task needs refinement.Ambiguity in task specifications becomes noise in metrics. Each task should be passable by an agent that follows instructions correctly. For each task, it’s useful to create a reference solution: a known working output that passes all graders.
Build balanced problem sets: Test both the cases where a behavior should occur and where it shouldn't. One-sided evals create one-sided optimization. When to refund for example.
Build a eval harness with a stable environment: It’s essential that the agent in the eval functions roughly the same as the agent used in production, and that the environment itself doesn’t introduce further noise. Each trial should be “isolated” by starting from a clean environment.
Design graders thoughtfully: We recommend choosing deterministic graders where possible, LLM graders where necessary or for additional flexibility, and using human graders judiciously for additional validation. It’s often better to grade what the agent produced, not the path it took. For tasks with multiple components, build in partial credit. A support agent that correctly identifies the problem and verifies the customer but fails to process a refund is meaningfully better than one that fails immediately. To avoid hallucinations in judges, give the LLM a way out, like providing an instruction to return “Unknown” when it doesn’t have enough information. Grade each dimension with an isolated LLM-as-judge rather than using one to grade all dimensions.
Check the traces: You won't know if your graders are working well unless you read the transcripts and grades from many trials. At Anthropic, we invested in tooling for viewing eval transcripts and we regularly take the time to read them. When a task fails, the transcript tells you whether the agent made a genuine mistake or whether your graders rejected a valid solution.

Defining eval tasks is one of the best ways to stress-test whether the product requirements are concrete enough to start building. #### Evals aren't the only test
A complete picture includes production monitoring, user feedback, A/B testing, manual transcript review, and systematic human evaluation. Automated evals are especially useful pre-launch and in CI/CD, running on each agent change and model upgrade as the first line of defense against quality problems. Production monitoring kicks in post-launch to detect distribution drift and unanticipated real-world failures. A/B testing validates significant changes once you have sufficient traffic. User feedback and transcript review are ongoing practices to fill the gaps: triage feedback constantly, sample transcripts to read weekly, and dig deeper as needed.

## Evaluation FAQ
It’s important to recognize that evaluation is part of the development process rather than a distinct line item, similar to how debugging is part of software development. You should always be doing error analysis. In the projects we’ve worked on, we’ve spent 60-80% of our development time on error analysis and evaluation. Expect most of your effort to go toward understanding failures (i.e. looking at data) rather than building automated checks.
Where to Start: Start from error analysis then go to evaluation. Your evaluation strategy should emerge from observed failure patterns (e.g. error analysis), not predetermined query classifications. Consider the cost hierarchy of different evaluator types. Simple assertions and reference-based checks (comparing against known correct answers) are cheap to build and maintain. LLM-as-Judge evaluators require 100+ labeled examples, ongoing weekly maintenance, and coordination between developers, PMs, and domain experts. This cost difference should shape your evaluation strategy.
Only build expensive evaluators for problems you’ll iterate on repeatedly. Since LLM-as-Judge comes with significant overhead, save it for persistent generalization failures - not issues you can fix trivially. Start with cheap code-based checks where possible: regex patterns, structural validation, or execution tests. Reserve complex evaluation for subjective qualities that can’t be captured by simple rules.
Search: You have multiple retrieval strategies available, ranging from simple keyword matching to embedding similarity to LLM-powered relevance filtering. The optimal approach depends on your specific use case, data characteristics, and performance requirements. Many production systems combine multiple strategies or use multi-hop retrieval guided by LLM agents. RAG systems have two distinct components that require different evaluation approaches: retrieval and generation.
Model: “I suggest not thinking of switching model as the main axes of how to improve your system off the bat without evidence. Does error analysis suggest that your model is the problem?”
Chunking: Avoid adding unrelated context. consider model, output type, task type and information type for better chunking. Consider map reduce type of solution. When sizing your chunks, try to respect content boundaries like paragraphs, sections, or chapters.
Label Desing: Start with binary labels to understand what ‘bad’ looks like. Numeric labels are advanced and usually not necessary. Start with a single benevolent dictator as your annotator whenever feasible. Only add complexity when your domain demands it.
### Q: How do I debug multi-turn conversation traces?
Start simple. Check if the whole conversation met the user’s goal with a pass/fail judgment. Look at the entire trace and focus on the first upstream failure. Read the user-visible parts first to understand if something went wrong. Only then dig into the technical details like tool calls and intermediate steps.
When you find a failure, reproduce it with the simplest possible test case. Here’s a example: suppose a shopping bot gives the wrong return policy on turn 4 of a conversation. Before diving into the full multi-turn complexity, simplify it to a single turn: “What is the return window for product X1000?” If it still fails, you’ve proven the error isn’t about conversation context - it’s likely a basic retrieval or knowledge issue you can debug more easily.
For generating test cases, you have two main approaches. First, you can simulate users with another LLM to create realistic multi-turn conversations. Second, use “N-1 testing” where you provide the first N-1 turns of a real conversation and test what happens next. The N-1 approach often works better since it uses actual conversation prefixes rather than fully synthetic interactions (but is less flexible and doesn’t test the full conversation). User simulation is getting better as models improve. Keep an eye on this space.

#### Takeways And Why Evals Are Essential
Evals are difficult to implement for the same reason that building great products is difficult; they require rigor, vision, and taste. If done well, evals become unique differentiators. In a world where information is freely available across the world and expertise is democratized, your advantage hinges on how well your systems can execute inside your context. At their core, evals are about a deep understanding of business context and objectives. If you cannot define what “great” means for your use case, you’re unlikely to achieve it.

### Best Practices and Common Pitfalls: 

- Obsess over measurement and iteration, not just the latest tools or frameworks.
- Don't ignore your data: Make reviewing traces and examples easy and routine. every day for all the team.
- Use specific, task-relevant evals: Generic evals (like toxicity) are insufficient.
- Align your LLM judge carefully and monitor its agreement with human experts continuously.
- Prefer pass/fail + reasoning from judges over simple numerical scores for richer feedback.
- Start simple and iterate; don't over-engineer your evaluation suite from day one.
- Beware of benchmark hacking: Focus on performance on your specific tasks and data, not just leaderboard scores.
- Integrate user feedback loops, but be mindful of selection bias; use it for insights and examples, not solely for aggregate metrics.
- Focusing only on tools without understanding the underlying evaluation processes leads to failure.
